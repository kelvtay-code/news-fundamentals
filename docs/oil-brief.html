Headline Context

Yemen's Houthi militants attacked the Saudi-flagged crude tanker Layla and the products tanker Encelia in the Red Sea on July 23 — the first Houthi-linked attacks on Red Sea shipping in a year — opening a second major chokepoint (Bab al-Mandab) on top of an already-restricted Strait of Hormuz. The Encelia fire was confirmed by Saudi Press Agency (crew safe); Iran has separately told the Houthis to "stand ready to close the Red Sea oil route" should the US strike Iranian infrastructure, and President Trump has vowed to bomb an Iranian bridge or power plant every time Iran fires on a ship in Hormuz. ICE September Brent settled $100.69/b (+$6.62, +7%) — above $100/b for the first time since May 22 — while NYMEX WTI settled $92.19/b (+$5.36, +6.2%). Dated Brent hit a seven-week high of $104.92/b and cash Dubai jumped 8.7% to $97.19/b, with the Murban-Dubai premium surging $17.58/b to $24.98/b (highest since April 7) as undisrupted light-sour barrels are bid up hard. Backwardation exploded: sixth-month ICE Brent's discount to front-month widened to $17.48/b (widest since May 18) and the equivalent NYMEX WTI spread hit $15.47/b (widest since May 20) — a classic near-term-tightness signal.

Layered on top: Kazakhstan cut oil output after repeated attacks on tankers loading at the Caspian Pipeline Consortium (CPC) terminal at Novorossiysk forced loadings to be suspended (briefly resumed July 20, then suspended again) — UBS estimates up to 1.8 million b/d of Kazakh crude could be at risk if the disruption persists, though the ministry says all CPC infrastructure remains "in good working order" and ready to resume once conditions normalize. TotalEnergies warned the Middle East war could keep 5%-10% of its oil and gas output at risk while hostilities persist, having already absorbed an 8% annual production loss (Q2 output 2.4 million boe/d, down from 2.55 million boe/d) and a 12% refinery-throughput decline (SATORP drone-attack damage, Donges planned maintenance, Port Arthur lightning strike) — CEO Patrick Pouyanne called the Gulf situation "extremely volatile" and floated a "new normal" of the strait opening and closing with the tension cycle. Separately, Rhine water levels at the Kaub chokepoint were near 60cm late July 23 and falling toward ~43cm within 24 hours, with WSV forecasting a sub-20cm low by Aug 2-6 — potentially the lowest levels on record, threatening to halt barge navigation entirely and already forcing LyondellBasell to declare force majeure on its Wesseling butadiene unit. European 10ppm diesel FOB ARA barge was assessed $1,319.75/mt, highest since April 9.

Refinery / Cracker Outages

Unplanned, high-weight: TotalEnergies' 465,000 b/d SATORP refinery (joint venture with Saudi Aramco) suffered losses after a drone attack, pulling the company's overall refinery utilization down from 92% in Q1 to 80% in Q2 (1.4 million b/d average throughput, down from 1.6 million b/d) — Q3 utilization is guided at 80%-85% assuming SATORP recovers from ~70% to full capacity by quarter-end. Port Arthur (US) was also struck by lightning during a tropical storm (unplanned); France's 220,000 b/d Donges refinery underwent planned maintenance. Partially offsetting: Norway's Equinor made a new satellite discovery (7.6-10.5 million bbl) 6km north of the recovering Johan Castberg field, which itself was back online after an 18-day outage (Q3 production impact just 14,000 b/d) — a modest bullish-for-longevity, near-term-neutral data point.

Geopolitical & Policy Factors

The Houthi attack on the Layla (crude VLCC, Bahri-managed) and Encelia (products tanker, Bihar International-managed) on July 23 is a fresh (Day 1), high-weight, MOC-confirmed shock — the first Houthi attack on Red Sea shipping since July 2025. Iran has told the Houthis to "stand ready to close the Red Sea oil route" if the US strikes Iranian infrastructure; President Trump has vowed a US strike on an Iranian bridge or power plant for every Iranian shot fired at a ship in Hormuz — an active, escalating policy-and-military feedback loop. Separately, Kazakhstan's Energy Ministry confirmed a "controlled adjustment" (cut) to oil output after CPC loadings at Novorossiysk were suspended following attacks on tankers there (suspended, briefly resumed July 20, re-suspended) — UBS estimates up to 1.8 million b/d of Kazakh crude is at risk if the disruption extends, though the ministry stresses all CPC infrastructure remains intact and ready to resume immediately once conditions normalize. The EU also finalized its 21st Russia sanctions package July 23, freezing the oil price cap at $44.10/b for another year (rather than letting it float up >$10/b as market conditions would otherwise dictate) while carving out a 12-month Russian LNG transshipment allowance — a policy move that caps, rather than adds, price pressure.

Infrastructure / Execution Risk

Real tanker-flow data shows a genuine but still-porous disruption, not a clean shutdown: Hormuz crossings doubled to 20 ships July 22 from 16 the day before, but 16 of those 20 are Iran-linked/sanctioned, meaning normal commercial flow remains thin. Bab al-Mandab traffic actually rose to 44 ships (from 30), with over 60% linked to Suez/Egypt transits — "vessel movements through the waterway remained active despite heightened security concerns" (CAS). Crude/product flows completing full Bab al-Mandab transits averaged just 2.6 million b/d over July 1-20 versus 5.5 million b/d in June, and at least five tankers that departed Yanbu bound south have altered course — yet two VLCCs (Xin Long Yang, Cosnew Lake) that loaded at Yanbu were tracked transiting Bab al-Mandab on July 23 en route to China, a live test of whether Saudi exports can keep moving. Should VLCCs be forced onto the SUMED-pipeline/Suez workaround, voyage times would stretch from 13-19 days to 40+ days — a real but gradual capacity drain, not an instant flood of stranded barrels. The CPC network is reported "technically ready to resume normal operations" the moment security conditions allow, meaning any Kazakh recovery, once security clears, should be faster than a typical infrastructure-repair ramp — but recurrence risk (this is at least the second suspension in weeks) argues for treating the disruption as intermittent rather than resolved.

Tender Activity

No East-of-Suez LPG buy or sell tenders were reported this issue — a quiet week for the tender channel specifically, meaning this pillar is effectively silent rather than bullish or bearish.

Refinery / Cracker Outages

No LPG-specific refinery or cracker outage reported this issue in Asia. In Europe, LyondellBasell declared force majeure (effective July 16, unplanned) on its butadiene extraction unit at Wesseling, Germany — directly attributed to Rhine water levels being too low to deliver sufficient naphtha/LPG feedstock to steam crackers, curbing ethylene/propylene/crude-C4/butadiene output.

Geopolitical & Policy Factors

Marine insurance premiums for Strait of Hormuz transits have risen to 7.5%-10% of full cargo value, creating a genuine two-tier freight market (risk-averse operators sitting outside the strait, others attempting shuttle runs) — a direct cost-of-carry inflator for LPG cargoes moving through the Gulf. In the Black Sea, Ukraine's Ministry of Defense said its forces struck 10 cargo ships, one oil tanker and two tugboats in the prior 48 hours (196 vessels struck since July 6), and Romania flagged a possible LPG tanker attack July 20 — adding a second, distinct maritime-risk theater relevant to European LPG/naphtha logistics.

Infrastructure / Execution Risk

Rhine water levels are the dominant European logistics constraint: levels at Kaub were near 60cm late July 23 and forecast to fall to ~43cm within 24 hours, with a sub-20cm low (potentially an all-time record, below the 2018 record of 25cm) forecast for Aug 2-6. Below 40cm, large barges cannot operate at all — a binary, near-term supply-chain risk to LPG/naphtha deliveries into German/Swiss cracker sites that argues for treating any European LPG tightness as escalating over the next 7-10 days rather than already priced in.

Seasonality / Weather

Northwest Europe summer LPG demand remains subdued (comfortable inventories, ample availability), which is capping the pace of the price move despite the freight/Hormuz risk premium — a genuine demand-side offset to the supply story.

Flow Data

US propane and propylene inventories built 6.287 million barrels to 99.776 million barrels in the week ended July 17 — a new 2026 high and roughly 4x the 1.532 million barrel build S&P Global CERA analysts had projected — as US exports fell to their lowest level since July 2025. This is an explicit, EIA-confirmed demand miss on the US side even as Middle East-driven freight/supply risk pushes Asian and European cash prices higher; the Enterprise butane-propane spread in the US widened to ~44 cents/gal (back above 40 cents for the first time since May) as US butane is increasingly eyed as a replacement for curtailed Middle East supply, though recent US export softness reflects a currently weak arbitrage. West Mediterranean butane spiked $18/mt to $734/mt even as underlying fundamentals were explicitly described as "weak" (comfortable inventories, Morocco port congestion limiting spot flows) — a clean example of price tracking the broader energy complex higher while physical demand lags.

7-Day Consistency

The North Asia cargo uptrend (+$13/mt) is consistent with the broader crude/naphtha rally over the past several sessions, not a single-day spike. The AG differential weakening (-$9 → -$11/mt) and the US inventory build are both fresh, same-week data points that argue for tempering conviction rather than reinforcing it.

Bullish Factors

  • CFR North Asia/East China/South China cargoes all up $13-16/mt, tracking crude/naphtha higher
  • Hormuz marine insurance premiums up to 7.5%-10% of cargo value — genuine freight-cost inflation
  • Black Sea attacks (196 vessels struck since Jul 6) adding a second maritime risk theater
  • Rhine record-low water levels forcing LyondellBasell force majeure — European feedstock logistics genuinely constrained
  • US butane-propane spread widening on Middle East substitution demand

Bearish Factors

  • FOB AG propane/butane differentials to Saudi CP weakened to -$11/mt on explicit "lackluster demand" + healthy inventories
  • US propane/propylene stocks built to a 2026 high, ~4x consensus, as exports fell to a 1-year low
  • NWE summer LPG demand subdued; West Med fundamentals explicitly described as weak despite the price spike
  • Only 2 bids, zero trades in the North Asia cargo MOC — thin physical liquidity behind the price move

Rationale: Supply/freight (Hormuz war-risk premium, Black Sea attacks, Rhine-driven European feedstock constraints) and Market Structure (broad-based Asian cargo gains) both lean bullish, clearing the 2-pillar threshold, but Demand is explicitly soft on two separate continents (AG differential weakening, US inventory build far above forecast) — this is a crude/freight-cost-driven move, not a standalone LPG demand story. Held at Bullish rather than Extremely Bullish, with the demand-softness modifier reducing the implied magnitude of further upside and flagging that any Hormuz-risk premium could unwind faster than the crude complex if security conditions ease, given inventories are comfortable on both ends of the supply chain.
